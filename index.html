The Challenge

Standing Out in a Crowded Market

The biggest challenge was standing out in a crowded GenAI market filled with tools like MidJourney and Stable Diffusion WebUI. Building an internal GenAI product meant we had to deeply understand real user pain points inside game studios — not just build "another AI tool" but solve practical, daily production tasks with high usability.

After the initial MVP proved viability, I joined to own the full product lifecycle from Version 1 onward — translating business requirements into actionable specs, managing cross-functional teams, and driving the platform from early adoption to company-wide production tool.

Key PM decisions:

• Focused on solving specific studio workflows rather than building a generic AI tool

• Prioritized simplicity — most complex AI tools fail because users can't navigate them

• Targeted internal users first (marketers, designers, game studios) before external expansion

• Designed dual modes (Basic/Advanced) to serve both non-technical and power users

Product Strategy

Key Decisions & Approach

V2 UX
Simplified complex parameters for mass adoption. V1 showed all AI settings to prove depth; V2 hid complexity behind pre-set configs. Users went from tweaking 20+ parameters to generating production-ready assets in 3 clicks — without losing quality.
Templates
Built workflow-specific templates based on real studio needs. Instead of generic "text-to-image", created templates for Change Background, Change Skin, AI Sticker, Item Generation — tasks studios do daily. This shifted Artian from an experiment to a production tool.
Data
Established data-driven decision framework. Every feature required defined success criteria before development — covering acceptance criteria, success rate, adoption rate, and usage metrics. Added in-app rating system to feed user feedback directly into model improvement.
Scale
Scaled platform to 38 game titles with 100 LoRA-trained models for 17 titles — enabling game-specific AI generation at scale while maintaining 0.2% error rate.
Adoption
Drove adoption through campaigns and contests. Organized internal AI image contests, launched AI Avatar campaign that went viral across the company, and created mini app demos to showcase capabilities to game studios.

Reflections

What I Learned

01

Solve real workflows, not generic problems

The GenAI market is crowded with tools that do everything. Artian won internal adoption by focusing on specific studio tasks — Change Background, AI Sticker, Item Generation — not by being "another MidJourney". Specificity beats generality for enterprise products.

02

Simplicity is the real product challenge

V1 showed all parameters to prove depth. V2 hid them to prove maturity. The hardest PM decision was removing features users "could" use so they'd actually use what matters. Adoption jumped when we stopped treating users like AI engineers.

03

Platform products require ecosystem thinking

Scaling from 1 title to 38 required thinking about the platform as a service — consistent APIs, reusable LoRA models, and workflow templates that different studios could customize without engineering support.

04

Adoption is a product, not a launch

Features don't drive adoption — campaigns do. AI image contests, AI Avatar viral campaigns, and mini app demos converted skeptics into daily users. We treated internal adoption with the same rigor as external user acquisition.

Reflections

What I Learned

01

Compliance is a product opportunity

PDPL25 could have been a blocker — instead it became a chance to improve user trust and reduce notification costs. How you frame regulation determines team energy.

02

Cost optimization needs product thinking

The ZNS implementation wasn't just a tech migration — it required understanding user behavior, delivery reliability, and fallback strategy as product decisions.

03

Success criteria prevent scope creep

Defining what "done" means before writing code aligned the entire team. It's harder to gold-plate when everyone agreed on the finish line upfront.

04

Platform PMs need stakeholder empathy

Game teams depend on login infrastructure but often don't understand its constraints. Proactive communication on progress, risks, and outcomes builds trust.
